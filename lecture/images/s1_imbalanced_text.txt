[416] (Normal) Now, let’s look at some of the SMOTE variants, such as Borderline-SMOTE, SMOTE-NC, and SMOTEN. These variants apply the SMOTE algorithm to samples of a certain kind and may not always be applicable.
[417] (Normal) Borderline-SMOTE
[418] (Normal) Borderline-SMOTE [4] is a variation of SMOTE that generates synthetic samples from the minority class samples that are near the classification boundary, which divides the majority class from the minority class.
[419] (Normal) Why consider samples on the classification boundary?
[420] (Normal) The idea is that the examples near the classification boundary are more prone to misclassification than those far away from the decision boundary. Producing more such minority samples along the boundary would help the model learn better about the minority class. Intuitively, it is also true that the
[421] (Normal) Here’s a step-by-step algorithm for Borderline-SMOTE:
[422] (Normal) We run a KNN algorithm over the whole dataset.
[423] (Normal) Then, we divide the minority class points into three categories:
[424] (Normal) Noise points are minority class examples that have all the neighbors from the majority class. These points are buried among majority-class neighbors. They are likely outliers and can safely be ignored as “noise.”
[425] (Normal) Safe points have more minority-class neighbors than majority-class neighbors. Such observations don’t contain much information and can be safely ignored.
[426] (Normal) Danger points have more majority-class neighbors than minority-class neighbors. This implies that such observations are on or close to the boundary between the two classes.
[427] (Normal) Then, we train a KNN model only on the minority class examples.
[428] (Normal) Finally, we apply the SMOTE algorithm to the Danger points. Note that the neighbors of these Danger points may or may not be marked as Danger.

[1046] (Normal) As we discussed in Chapter 1, Introduction to Data Imbalance in Machine Learning, the PR curve is generally preferred over ROC curves for imbalanced datasets when the positive class is more important than the negative class. [1047] (Normal) We want the maximum possible values for both precision and recall, ideally both being 1. Thus, the point of optimality on a PR curve is (1,1).
[1048] (Normal) When we move away from this point of optimality, both the precision and recall values decrease, and we are less optimal.
[1049] (Normal) One measure that captures this trade-off between precision and recall is the F1 score. The F1 score is defined as the harmonic mean of the precision and recall:
[1050] (Normal) [IMG]F1 = 2 * (Precision * Recall) / (Precision + Recall)
[1051] (Normal) If we analyze this equation, we will see that the F1 score is highest (reaching its maximum at 1) when both precision and recall are 1, which is exactly the optimal point we defined on the PR curve.
[1052] (Normal) Therefore, optimizing for the maximum F1 score would ensure that we are striving to maximize both precision and recall, effectively pushing us toward the optimal point on the PR curve.
